3. Interpretation of the results

3.1) Compare the marginal mortality rates (2.1) with the mortality rates stratified by age group (2.2), and interpret the observed differences.

Interpretation of Simpson’s Paradox

If we examine the marginal mortality rates, we observe that they suggest a higher risk of death among vaccinated individuals (0.4107%) compared to unvaccinated individuals (0.1675%).

However, when we analyze mortality rates within each age group, this relationship reverses. Among individuals aged 50 or above, unvaccinated individuals have a higher mortality risk (5.9593%) compared to vaccinated individuals (1.6845%). Similarly, among individuals under 50, the mortality risk is also higher for unvaccinated individuals (0.0325%) than for vaccinated individuals (0.0234%).

This apparent paradox can be explained by differences in the age distribution between the two groups. Individuals aged 50 or above, who are naturally at higher risk of death regardless of vaccination status, are more present in the vaccinated group compared to the unvaccinated group.

Age distribution within each vaccination group

The figure shows that individuals aged 50 or above represent 23.32% of the vaccinated group, compared to only 2.28% of the unvaccinated group.

Therefore, when comparing marginal mortality rates, we are effectively comparing groups that are not similarly balanced in age. As a result, the marginal rates reflect not only the effect of vaccination, but also the effect of age.

Mathematical explanation

Unvaccinated group

\[ \begin{aligned} &\mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Unvaccinated})\\ &= \mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Unvaccinated, Under 50}) \cdot \color{blue}{\mathbb{P}(\text{Under 50} \mid \text{Unvaccinated})} + \mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Unvaccinated, 50+}) \cdot \color{red}{\mathbb{P}(\text{50+} \mid \text{Unvaccinated})} \\ &= 0.0325\% \cdot \color{blue}{0.9772} + 5.9593\% \cdot \color{red}{0.0228} \\ &\approx 0.1675\% \end{aligned} \]

Vaccinated group

\[ \begin{aligned} &\mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Vaccinated})\\ &= \mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Vaccinated, Under 50}) \cdot \color{blue}{\mathbb{P}(\text{Under 50} \mid \text{Vaccinated})} + \mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Vaccinated, 50+}) \cdot \color{red}{\mathbb{P}(\text{50+} \mid \text{Vaccinated})} \\ &= 0.0234\% \cdot \color{blue}{0.7668} + 1.6845\% \cdot \color{red}{0.2332} \\ &\approx 0.4107\% \end{aligned} \]

Focusing on the second term in each sum (which corresponds to individuals aged 50 or above), we observe that, although the mortality risk is higher among unvaccinated individuals:

\[ \mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Unvaccinated, 50+}) > \mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Vaccinated, 50+}) \quad (5.9593\% > 1.6845\%) \]

the weight associated with \(\mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Unvaccinated, 50+})\) is much smaller than the weight associated with \(\mathbb{P}(\text{Dying} \mid \text{Vaccinated, 50+})\):

\[ \mathbb{P}(\text{50+} \mid \text{Vaccinated}) = 0.2332 > 0.0228 = \mathbb{P}(\text{50+} \mid \text{Unvaccinated}) \]

This imbalance explains the reversal of the effect when comparing groups marginally versus within age strata.

Part 2. Propensity Score Weighting Analysis

To perform the propensity score weighting analysis, we will use real data from the NHEFS2 (National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey Data I Epidemiologic Follow-up Study).

Our goal is to estimate the average causal effect of smoking cessation (the treatment, A) on weight gain (the outcome, Y). To do so, we use data from 1,566 cigarette smokers aged 25 to 74 years who had a baseline visit (1971–1975) and a follow-up visit approximately 10 years later (1982).

Individuals were classified as treated (A = 1) if they reported having quit smoking before the follow-up visit, and as untreated (A = 0) otherwise. Weight gain Y was defined as the difference in body weight (in kg) between the follow-up and baseline visits.

For this analysis, we will mainly use the PSweight package3, which supports a range of propensity score weighting methods including inverse probability weighting (IPW), matching weights, overlap weights, etc.

The package is structured around two modules. The design module provides diagnostics to assess the adequacy of the propensity score model. The analysis module then provides functions to estimate causal effects under the chosen weighting scheme.

1.2) Using port function from RISCA package, check the positivity assumption.

The PoRT algorithm (\(\boldsymbol{PO}\)sitivity-\(\boldsymbol{R}\)egression \(\boldsymbol{T}\)ree) is a function in the RISCA package designed to identify potential positivity violations. It relies on regression trees to search for subgroups of individuals where the treatment probability is close to 0.

To illustrate how it works, consider a simple regression tree predicting the treatment A from a single covariate, sex:

This tree predicts the probability of quitting smoking (\(A=1\)) based on sex. For females (sex = 1), the estimated probability of being treated is 0.23. For males (sex = 0), it is 0.29.

Recall that positivity requires that every subgroup (male or female) present in the population has a non-zero probability of receiving each level of the treatment (\(A=0\) and \(A=1\)):

\[ \mathbb{P}(\text{Male}) > 0 \implies 0 < \mathbb{P}(A=1 \mid \text{Male}) < 1 \] \[ \mathbb{P}(\text{Female}) > 0 \implies 0 < \mathbb{P}(A=1 \mid \text{Female}) < 1 \]

So we must first check that the subgroups of males and females are sufficiently represented in the population. This can be done through the argument alpha of the port() function, which represents the minimum proportion of the total sample that a subgroup must represent to be considered, and is equal to 0.05 by default. Here, \(\mathbb{P}(\text{Male}) = 0.49 > 0.05\) and \(\mathbb{P}(\text{Female}) = 0.51 > 0.05\), so both subgroups are sufficiently represented.

Then, we check the proportion of treated in each subgroup. This is done through the argument beta, which represents the treatment probability below which a positivity violation is suspected, and is equal to 0.05 by default. Here, \(\mathbb{P}(A=1 \mid \text{Male}) = 0.29 > 0.05\) and \(\mathbb{P}(A=1 \mid \text{Female}) = 0.23 > 0.05\), so no positivity violation is identified.

We can confirm this by running the port() function on all covariates:

library(RISCA)

port(group = "A",
     cov.quanti = c("age", "smokeintensity", "smokeyears", "weightbaseline"),
     cov.quali = c("sex", "race", "education", "active", "exercise"),
     data = nhefs, alpha = 0.05, beta = 0.05, gamma = 1)
## No potential positivity violation identified.

The group argument is a character string specifying the name of the treatment variable in the data: 0 for untreated patients and 1 for treated patients.

The gamma parameter controls how many covariates can be combined to define a subgroup. With \(\gamma = 1\), the algorithm builds one tree per covariate separately, as in the example above. With \(\gamma = 2\), it would additionally build trees using all possible pairs of covariates (e.g., sex and race together), allowing it to detect violations in more specific subgroups. We set \(\gamma = 1\) here because increasing it tends to produce very small subgroups that may reflect random fluctuations rather than meaningful positivity concerns.

2. Inverse probability weighting (IPW)

The PSweight package is built on a general framework for propensity score weighting that unifies several weighting schemes — including inverse probability weighting (IPW), matching weights, overlap weights, and entropy weights — under a single formulation known as balancing weights.

The key idea is the following. Let \(f(x)\) denote the density of the adjustment covariates \(X = (\text{sex, age, race, ...})\) in the overall population. In an observational study, the distribution of these covariates typically differs between the treated and untreated groups, which prevents a direct comparison of outcomes. The goal of propensity score weighting is to reweight individuals so that the covariate distributions become balanced across treatment groups, thereby making them comparable.

For example, inverse probability weighting (IPW) ensures that the covariates \(X\) become independent of the treatment \(A\) in the weighted population, i.e., \(f(X \mid A=1) = f(X \mid A=0) = f(X)\). In this case, the target population is the original population in which \(X\) is distributed according to \(f(X)\), and IPW reweights both treatment groups so that they resemble this target population in terms of the covariate distribution.

More generally, the density of \(X\) in the target population is defined as \(h(x)f(x)\), where \(h(x)\) is called the tilting function. Different choices of \(h(x)\) correspond to different weighting schemes:

  • IPW: \(h(x) = 1\) (targets the entire population)
  • Treated weights: \(h(x) = e(x)\) (targets the treated subpopulation)
  • Overlap weights: \(h(x) = e(x)(1 - e(x))\) (targets the subpopulation with the most overlap between groups)
  • Matching weights: \(h(x) = \min(e(x), 1 - e(x))\)

where \(e(x) = \mathbb{P}(A=1 \mid X=x)\) is the propensity score.

The causal estimand associated with each weighting scheme is the weighted average treatment effect (WATE), defined as:

\[ \tau_h = \frac{\mathbb{E}[h(X)\{m_1(X) - m_0(X)\}]}{\mathbb{E}[h(X)]} \]

where \(m_1(x) = \mathbb{E}(Y(1) \mid X=x)\) and \(m_0(x) = \mathbb{E}(Y(0) \mid X=x)\) are the conditional potential outcome means under treatment and control, respectively. When \(h(x) = 1\), this reduces to the standard average treatment effect (ATE).

2.1) Construct the exposure model using multivariate logistic regression, and extract the propensity score for each individual

The SumStat function from the PSweight package fits a propensity score model and computes summary statistics for covariate balance assessment. The main arguments are:

  • ps.formula: the propensity score model specified as a formula. The left-hand side is the treatment variable (\(A\)), and the right-hand side lists the covariates used to predict treatment assignment.
  • method: the estimation method for the propensity score. "glm" fits a logistic regression (generalized linear model with binomial family).
  • data: the data frame containing all the variables referenced in the formula.
  • weight: the weighting scheme to apply. "IPW" stands for inverse probability weighting, which targets the ATE in the entire population (tilting function \(h(x) = 1\)). Other options include "overlap", "matching", "entropy", and "treated".

2.2) Check the positivity assumption by plotting the distribution of the propensity score in the two separate groups of treatment.

Recall that checking the positivity assumption amounts to verifying that, in each covariate subgroup, there are enough individuals in both treatment groups to estimate the conditional mean \(\mathbb{E}(Y \mid A, X)\).

As seen in the course, the propensity score theorem states that if conditional exchangeability holds — i.e., \(Y(a) \perp A \mid X\) — then it also holds conditional on the propensity score alone:

\[ Y(a) \perp A \mid e(X) \]

where \(e(X) = \mathbb{P}(A=1 \mid X=x)\) is a scalar summary of the possibly high-dimensional covariate vector \(X\). This means that instead of needing to balance all covariates simultaneously, it suffices to condition on the single number \(e(X)\).

Therefore, we can estimate \(\mathbb{E}(Y \mid A, e(X))\) instead of \(\mathbb{E}(Y \mid A, X)\), which is far more practical. But for this to work, we need enough treated and untreated individuals at each value of \(e(X)\) — which is precisely what the propensity score distribution plot allows us to verify. Where the distributions of \(e(X)\) in the two treatment groups overlap, the comparison is feasible; where they do not, the causal effect is not identifiable.

The PSweight package provides a plot function to visualize the distribution of the estimated propensity scores in both treatment groups. This allows us to assess the positivity assumption by checking whether the distributions overlap.

We can first display the distributions as a histogram:

# Histogram of propensity scores by treatment group

plot(IPW_Model, type = "hist", breaks = 50)

The histogram shows that both the untreated group (0) and the treated group (1) share a common range of propensity scores, approximately from 0.05 to 0.65. The untreated group has taller bars because it contains more individuals (1163 vs 403). The untreated group peaks at slightly lower propensity scores than the treated group, which is expected since their covariate profiles are associated with a somewhat lower predicted probability of quitting. A few treated individuals have propensity scores above 0.7, where almost no untreated individuals are found — this represents a region of limited overlap, but it concerns very few observations. Overall, the overlap is sufficient and the positivity assumption is reasonably satisfied.

2.4) Compute (extract) the inverse probability weights for each individual.

The IPW weights are defined by:

\[ w_i = \frac{A_i}{e(X_i)} + \frac{1 - A_i}{1 - e(X_i)} \]

where \(A_i\) is the treatment indicator and \(e(X_i) = \mathbb{P}(A=1 \mid X_i)\) is the propensity score for subject \(i\). For treated individuals (\(A_i = 1\)), the weight reduces to \(\frac{1}{e(X_i)}\), and for untreated individuals (\(A_i = 0\)), it reduces to \(\frac{1}{1 - e(X_i)}\).

2.5) Check the goodness-of-fit of the propensity score model by calculating the absolute standardized difference (ASD).

A rule of thumb for determining adequate balance is when the ASD of all covariates is controlled within 0.1.

The ASD is defined as:

\[ ASD = \left| \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_1(\mathbf{x}_i) Z_i X_{pi}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_1(\mathbf{x}_i) Z_i} - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_0(\mathbf{x}_i)(1 - Z_i) X_{pi}}{\sum_{i=1}^{N} w_0(\mathbf{x}_i)(1 - Z_i)} \right| \bigg/ \sqrt{\frac{s_1^2 + s_0^2}{2}} \]

where \((w_0, w_1)\) are the balancing weights, \(Z_i\) is the treatment indicator, \(X_{pi}\) is the \(p\)-th covariate for individual \(i\), and \(s_z^2\) is the variance of the \(p\)-th covariate in group \(z\). Setting \(w_0 = w_1 = 1\) corresponds to the unweighted mean differences.

Goodness-of-fit of the propensity score model is usually assessed based on the resulting covariate balance. In the context of propensity score weighting, this is measured using the absolute standardized difference (ASD). We compare the ASD before and after weighting using the summary() function:

# type = "balance": returns covariate means and balance metrics by treatment group
# weighted.var: if TRUE, uses weighted variances in the ASD denominator
# metric: "ASD" for absolute standardized difference, or "PSD" for population standardized difference

3. Matching

Matching weights

The matching weights define a target population composed of paired individuals. The idea is the following: for each treated individual with covariates \(X = x\), we randomly select an untreated individual with the same covariates \(X = x\), and vice versa. At each level \(X = x\), the number of pairs that can be constructed is equal to the minimum of the two group sizes: the number of treated and the number of untreated at that covariate value.

Therefore, the density of \(X\) in the target (matched) population is:

\[ f_{\text{matched}}(x) \propto f(x) \cdot \min\{e(x), 1 - e(x)\} \]

which corresponds to the tilting function \(h(x) = \min\{e(x), 1 - e(x)\}\).

Intuition: in the matched population, for each covariate value \(x\), the representation \(P(X = x)\) in the target population is proportional to \(f(x)\) times the probability of belonging to the smaller group. For example, if at \(X = x\) there are 10 treated and 5 untreated individuals (i.e., \(e(x) > 1 - e(x)\)), then only 5 pairs can be formed. The matched population at this point reflects the size of the smaller group:

\[ f_{\text{matched}}(x) \propto f(x)(1 - e(x)) = P(X = x) \cdot P(A = 0 \mid X = x) = P(X = x, A = 0) \]

The corresponding balancing weights are:

\[ w_1(x) = \frac{\min\{e(x), 1 - e(x)\}}{e(x)}, \quad w_0(x) = \frac{\min\{e(x), 1 - e(x)\}}{1 - e(x)} \]

Intuition behind the weights: the group with fewer individuals at a given \(x\) receives a weight of 1 — all its members will be matched. The larger group receives a weight less than 1, down-weighting it to match the size of the smaller group.

To see this, suppose at \(X = x\) the treated group is larger than the untreated group (\(e(x) > 1 - e(x)\)). Then:

\[ w_0(x) = \frac{1 - e(x)}{1 - e(x)} = 1 \quad \text{(all untreated are kept)} \]

\[ w_1(x) = \frac{1 - e(x)}{e(x)} < 1 \quad \text{(treated are down-weighted)} \]

We can verify that the weights balance the covariate distributions toward the matched target population. The density of \(X\) among the treated is \(f_1(x) = P(X = x \mid A = 1) \propto f(x) \cdot e(x)\). After weighting:

\[ f_1(x) \cdot w_1(x) \propto f(x) \cdot e(x) \cdot \frac{1 - e(x)}{e(x)} = f(x)(1 - e(x)) \]

Similarly, the density of \(X\) among the untreated is \(f_0(x) \propto f(x)(1 - e(x))\). After weighting:

\[ f_0(x) \cdot w_0(x) \propto f(x)(1 - e(x)) \cdot \frac{1 - e(x)}{1 - e(x)} = f(x)(1 - e(x)) \]

Both weighted distributions are equal to \(f(x)(1 - e(x))\), confirming that the matching weights balance the covariates toward the matched target population — the subpopulation defined by the smaller group at each covariate value.

4. Overlap weights

Overlap weights

The overlap weights define a target population that emphasizes individuals who could have plausibly been assigned to either treatment group — those in the region of clinical equipoise. The tilting function is \(h(x) = e(x)(1 - e(x))\), which peaks when the propensity score is close to 0.5 and vanishes when it is close to 0 or 1.

The density of \(X\) in the overlap target population is therefore:

\[ f_{\text{overlap}}(x) \propto f(x) \cdot e(x)(1 - e(x)) \]

The corresponding balancing weights are:

\[ w_1(x) = \frac{e(x)(1 - e(x))}{e(x)} = 1 - e(x), \quad w_0(x) = \frac{e(x)(1 - e(x))}{1 - e(x)} = e(x) \]

Intuition: each treated individual is weighted by the probability of not being treated, and each untreated individual is weighted by the probability of being treated. Individuals with propensity scores near 0.5 receive the highest weights, while those with extreme scores (near 0 or 1) are naturally down-weighted. Unlike IPW, the overlap weights are always bounded between 0 and 1, which avoids the problem of extreme weights.

Optimality property: The overlap weights minimize the asymptotic variance of the weighted average treatment effect estimator among the entire class of balancing weights.
